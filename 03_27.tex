      \textbf{Application Layer}
      \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{File writer} --- parallel multi-format raw writes
        \item \textbf{Network manager} --- auto-detect ports, configure on boot
        \item \textbf{Orchestrator} --- central controller
        \item \textbf{Projection engine} --- custom CUDA kernels
        \item \textbf{Web GUI} --- WebSocket server to browser
      \end{itemize}

  \end{columns}

% SECTION 3: FLIR M364C DRIVER REWRITE

\sectionsubtitle{Threads are fun. . .}
\section{FLIR M364C Driver Rewrite}

% --- Slide 9: What Broke ---
\begin{frame}{What Broke \& Why}
  \textbf{The Bug}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Original C++ driver used multiple writer threads but a \textit{single shared mutex}
    \item At low FPS / single format: contention was masked
    \item At higher FPS + multi-format writes (thermal + visible + radiometric): writers serialize on the lock
    \item Each writer held the mutex during disk I/O --- blocking all others
  \end{itemize}

  \vspace{0.5em}
  \textbf{Consequence}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Dropped frames from growing queues
    \item Memory leak --- queues grew unbounded when writers couldn't keep up
    \item System became unusable at target frame rates
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

% --- Slide 10: What Was Fixed ---
\begin{frame}{What Was Fixed}
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Per-writer threading} --- eliminated shared mutex during I/O; each writer has its own queue
    \item \textbf{UUIDv7 image referencing} --- each frame gets a unique, time-sortable UUID (RFC 9562); stored in EXIF \texttt{ImageUniqueID} and XMP \texttt{dc:Identifier}
    \item \textbf{Metadata in EXIF/XMP} --- moved metadata out of filenames and into the JPEG image data itself (emissivity, distance, palette, camera params)
    \item \textbf{Master metadata manifest} --- updated the central manifest file to index by UUID with full sensor parameters
  \end{enumerate}

  \vspace{0.5em}
  Both thermal and visible frames from the same capture share the same UUID for pairing.
\end{frame}

\section{Aligned Thermal/RGB Data}

% --- Slide 12: Two Experiments ---
\begin{frame}{Two Experiments}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}
      \textbf{Experiment 1: Physical Offset}
      \begin{itemize}
        \item Measured center-to-center lens separation: 6.5cm
        \item Static scene: moved entire rig by exactly the lens offset
        \item Effectively co-located both lenses at the same position across two captures
        \item Result: perfectly aligned thermal/RGB pairs
      \end{itemize}
    \end{column}
    \begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}
      \textbf{Experiment 2: Controlled Parallax}
      \begin{itemize}
        \item At 6.5cm baseline, parallax is sub-pixel beyond $\sim$200ft
        \item Setup: top of parking garage, objects at 200--500ft
        \item Entire frame is effectively parallax-free (aligned)
        \item Then moved camera sideways by several feet $\rightarrow$ synthetic misalignment
        \item Result: controlled pairs with known alignment/misalignment
      \end{itemize}
    \end{column}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}

\sectionsubtitle{Stability is hard to come by these days}
\section{Automated IMU Calibration Testing}

% --- Slide 14: The Approach ---
\begin{frame}{Horizon-Based Stabilization Eval.}
  \textbf{Problem:} How to quantify how un-level or unstable an IMU-stabilized camera is after ``stabilization''?

  \vspace{0.3em}
  \textbf{Method}
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item Semantic segmentation on water scenes $\rightarrow$ land/water/sky masks
    \item Extract water-sky boundary $\rightarrow$ RANSAC line fit
    \item Compute horizon angle per frame: $\theta = \arctan(\text{slope})$
    \item Track $\theta$ deviation over time $\rightarrow$ RMS deviation = stabilization quality
  \end{enumerate}

  \vspace{0.3em}
  \begin{table}
    \centering
    \footnotesize
    \begin{tabular}{lccc}
      \toprule
      \textbf{Method} & \textbf{Static RMS} & \textbf{Latency} & \textbf{Drift Handling} \\
      \midrule
      Exponential MA    & 1--5\textdegree   & 50--200ms & None \\
      Complementary     & 0.5--2\textdegree & 5--20ms   & Partial \\
      EKF/MEKF          & 0.1--0.5\textdegree & 1--5ms  & Full (bias est.) \\
      \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
  \end{table}

  \vspace{0.3em}
  \textbf{Status:} Early-stage / future work --- blocked on synced time data between camera and IMU.
\end{frame}

\sectionsubtitle{Maybe the machines can take my job too}
\section{VLM-Controlled Segmentation}

% --- Slide 16: The Problem with SAM ---
\begin{frame}{The Problem with SAM}
  \textbf{SAM 2 (Meta, 2024)} is state-of-the-art for promptable segmentation, but:

  \vspace{0.3em}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item When you click on different parts of the \textit{same object}, SAM refuses to grow the mask
    \item Instead it produces separate masks or ignores new clicks entirely
    \item \textbf{Root causes:}
    \begin{itemize}
      \item Decoder biased toward compact, semantically ``tight'' segments
      \item Training data (SA-1B): each mask = one coherent visual region
      \item Point prompts on distant regions confuse the decoder's attention
    \end{itemize}
    \item SAM 2.1 improves video tracking but does not fix this
    \item SAM 3 (Nov 2025) adds Promptable Concept Segmentation (text/image $\rightarrow$ all instances) but the per-object multi-click limitation persists
  \end{itemize}

  \vspace{0.3em}
  This is a known community pain point with no clean solution in the literature.
\end{frame}

% --- Slide 17: Our Approach ---
\begin{frame}{Sub-Masks + VLM Control}
  \textbf{Novel Contribution: Sub-Mask Union}
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item Click on each visually distinct part of the object separately
    \item Get individual sub-masks for each part from SAM
    \item Union all sub-masks $\rightarrow$ complete object mask
    \item Morphological closing to fill gaps
  \end{enumerate}

  \vspace{0.2em}
  Practiced informally in annotation tools but \textit{never formalized or evaluated systematically}.

  \vspace{0.3em}
  \textbf{VLM as Controller:} Qwen2-VL-8B (7.6B params) reasons about where to click, when to zoom, when to stop.

  \vspace{0.2em}
  \textbf{Current Results}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Beats SOTA models on edge cases (where SAM's single-click fails)
    \item Does not beat SOTA on average --- using \textit{untrained} models only
    \item \textbf{Next step:} RL training (GRPO) to learn tool-use policy (zoom, super-resolution, sub-mask creation)
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\section{Compute Acquisition}

% --- Slide 19: Why We Need Compute ---
\begin{frame}{Why I Need Compute}
  \textbf{Current Hardware:} 8$\times$ RTX 6000 Ada (48GB VRAM each)

  \vspace{0.3em}
  \textbf{RL Training Requirements for Qwen2-VL-8B}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item GRPO + LoRA: 2--4$\times$ A100 80GB, 50--200 GPU-hours
    \item Full PPO: 4--8$\times$ A100 80GB, 100--500 GPU-hours
    \item Multiple experiment iterations needed $\rightarrow$ cumulative demand is high
  \end{itemize}

  \vspace{0.3em}
  \textbf{The Gap}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item RTX 6000 Ada (48GB) can fit single-model inference and LoRA fine-tuning
    \item PPO requires $\sim$48GB just for weights (policy + value + reference) --- no room for activations
    \item GRPO needs $\sim$32GB (policy + reference) + activations $\rightarrow$ tight fit, limits batch size
    \item I need A100 80GB or H100 class hardware for full-scale RL training at reasonable throughput
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}
